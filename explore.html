Let's explore our Solar System first.

There is so much to learn in this area of the universe.

There are billions of stars that we now know have their own planets orbiting around. But this one we call Solar System, we can reach and explore.

This one is home !

Our Sun

At the center of our Solar System, the sun casts its light and warmth on us from 150 millions of kms.
It is a G-type star also called yellow dwarfs. While the temperature at the surface raise up to 5600°C, its core hits 15 millions °C.
The sun also rotates on itself in about 25 days.
Star Yellow dwarf

Mercury

Closest planet to the sun, the temperature at the surface facing our star averages 400°C while the 'night side' drops at -220°C.
Mercury orbits in 88 days around the sun, that's a very short year. But by rotating very slowly on it self, days lasts almost 59 earth days.
It is a rocky planet with no atmosphere. No sign of life is expected on this dried out out planet.
Rocky planet Inner Planet

Venus

Venus was previously known a the 'morning star' and 'evening star' being the first visible celestial body to appear in evening and the last to disappear at dawn.
By orbiting closer to the Sun than Earth (inner planet), it never appears distant from it.
Venus atmosphere consists mostly of carbon dioxide and nitrogen. Its temperature raises over 450°C while its atmospheric pressure is 91 times stronger than Earth's.
Rocky planet Inner Planet

Earth

The blue marble ! Our home planet is unique in many ways, in particular by being the only (known) planet in the universe to host life.
For a long time humans thought Earth as the center of the universe. We now know it orbits around the sun in 365,26 days while rotating on it self in 23 hours and 56 minutes.
Earth core creates a strong magnetic field which protect the atmosphere and surface from Solar wind & flares.
Rocky planet Home

Mars

First 'outer planet', meaning orbiting further away from the Sun than Earth, it is a rocky planet with a very thin atmosphere.
While there is no more liquid water on its surface, it is still present in the polar regions in solid form (ice).
Mars has many similarities with Earth. This is why we have faith in finding trace of life (current or past) on Mars.
Rocky Planet Outer Planet

Jupiter

Largest planet in our solar system, Jupiter's mass is equivalent to 2.5 times all other planets together.
Jupiter orbits at more than 778 millions of kms from the Sun in 4333 Earth days, that's 11.8 years.
It is called 'Gaz Giant' as it is composed mainly of gaz (and liquid). The atmosphere show impressive 'eternal' storms. They have been spotted almost 400years ago and are still spinning in different directions.
Gaz Giant Outer Planet

Saturn

Second largest planet of our Solar System, Saturn is mostly known for its beautiful rings.
Saturn holds the moon count record: 82 (with 53 being confirmed). Its largest, Titan, has shown great potential to host life forms.
While it is a gaz giant type, its core is made of metal and extra layer of rocky compound.
Gaz Giant Outer Planet

Uranus

Uranus is a frozen planet orbiting at 2821 millions of kms, meaning the Sun light needs almost 3h to reach its surface (compared to 8 minutes for Earth).
Also called the 'sideways planet' as its rotating axe is tilted (98°). This causes major change in the planet's seasons. The equator region experience day/night cycles lasting 9h while in the pole regions they last 42 Earth-years as Uranus orbits in 84 Earth-years.
Ice Giant Outer Planet

Neptune

Last planet of our solar system is Neptune orbiting at 4477 millions kms. It completes its orbit in over 165 Earth-years.
Its blue color is due to the composition of its atmosphere: mainly hydrogen, helium and methane.
Winds on Neptune can speed at over 2000km/h (3 times stronger than Jupiter's) despite its distance from the Sun and the low energy it receives from it.
Ice Giant Outer Planet
